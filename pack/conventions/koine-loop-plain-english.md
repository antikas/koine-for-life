# The Loop

Anything that matters, whether a decision, a plan, or a review, goes through four steps. You do not need to know they are called this. Claude follows the steps, and the user just has a conversation.

## The four steps

**1. Intent.** What are we actually trying to do, and why? What would "done" look like? What would we need to see to know we got it wrong?

**2. Action.** Do the thing. Write the notes. Make the decision. Capture what was spent, read, or planned.

**3. Review.** Look at what we just did. Is it clear? Are assumptions spelt out? Is there a concrete next step? Did we miss something?

**4. Log.** Write it down where future-us can find it. Not the whole conversation, just what we decided or changed.

## Why this matters

Most things go wrong between step 1 and step 2, when we jump into doing without being clear on why. Or between step 3 and step 4, when we decide something but never write it down, and three months later nobody remembers.

The loop feels slow the first few times and then fast. Most conversations run through it in under a minute.

## When to run the full loop

- Any decision the user would be annoyed to rediscover they had already made.
- Any plan with more than two or three steps.
- Any review (weekly, monthly, term, annual).
- Anything the user says "this is important" about.

## When the loop is overkill

- Quick factual questions.
- Chat that is not going anywhere in particular.
- Operations the user has done many times and just wants executed.

## Readiness vs. intent

A subtle one, worth naming. *Intent* is what we want to do. *Readiness* is whether we can actually do it yet. They come apart.

You can decide to buy a house (intent) well before the deposit, mortgage offer, and survey are in hand (readiness). Both are worth tracking separately, so that "we decided this" and "we can act on it" do not get confused.

When a decision is logged, note both: what we decided, and what has to be true before we act on it.
